02 Our Thesis

We're not automating the old shop model.
We're building what's next.

The capacity we're losing to closing businesses can't come back by simply training machinists faster or by moving quoting online.

The production capacity itself needs to be rebuilt.


A shop built around human judgment at every step can't economically, practically, or quickly be automated. The entire operation has to be designed for autonomy from first principles. That means rebuilding production capacity from the ground up, instead of making incremental patches to retrofit legacy job shops.


The hard part isn't the machines. It's knowing how to actually make parts. The feeds, the speeds, how a tools wear, how to avoid chatter, the job setup, the fixtures, the finishing. Every part we make compounds that knowledge.


NEXT isn't just another job shop trying to run faster. We're building the next generation of factories rebuilding America's production capacity. With a network of autonomous factories across the country, we absorb work from shops that are shutting down, take on overflow from those still running, and rapidly deliver high-quality machined components to every company that needs them.

03 The Window

Five forces are converging right now.

There's a narrow window to rebuild America's production base before we lose the knowledge and capacity to do so.

1

Reshoring mandate

National policy and market demand are pushing to Reindustrialize and rebuild America's production capacity.

2

Structural labor shortage

Skilled machinists are retiring faster than capable replacements can be trained. There's no quick and easy workforce solution.

3

Automation economics crossed the threshold

Hardware costs and enabling technology maturity finally make autonomous production achievable and commercially viable.

4

AI is a force multiplier

AI has unlocked new capabilities across multiple disciplines to do more with less, faster and better.

5

Unprecedented market consolidation

A wave of retiring shop owners will force billions in orphaned work to consolidate in fewer facilities. The question isn't if that happens, it's whether it consolidates into modern domestic facilities or gets shipped overseas.
