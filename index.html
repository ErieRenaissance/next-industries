Rebuilding America's Industrial Backbone

The American Manufacturing Gap: Hidden Potential in Plain Sight

America's industrial backbone is at a critical juncture. While headlines focus on technology disruption and digital transformation, a fundamental gap exists in how capital serves the manufacturing sector. Thousands of profitable, well-established industrial businesses across the American heartland find themselves in a structural bind, neither failing nor flourishing, but rather trapped in a capital no-man's-land that prevents them from reaching their full potential despite forming the core of American supply chains.

The Overlooked Manufacturing Middle: Too Small for PE, Too Tangible for VC

This gap affects a specific segment of the market: small and mid-sized industrial firms that are systematically overlooked by traditional capital providers: too mature for venture capital, too small and bespoke for private equity, and too operationally manual for bank financing. These companies share common characteristics: they are profitable but starved of growth capital; run by aging owners with no succession plans; operationally sound but technologically outdated; and located in regions underserved by private equity and overlooked by venture capital.

Our Focus: Fabricated Metals, Machinery, and Primary Metals (NAICS 331–333)

Our focus is on the Fabricated Metals, Machinery, and Primary Metals sectors (NAICS 331, 332, and 333), which form the bedrock of the U.S. industrial economy with more than 72,000 establishments nationwide. Despite their central role in economic competitiveness and supply chain resilience, many of these companies operate in isolation. They are often profitable but undercapitalized, technologically outdated, and lacking succession plans or access to aligned capital.

Current Capital Models: Misaligned Incentives, Missed Opportunities

Private equity often prioritizes short-term returns and quick exits.

Venture capital focuses on software and asset-light businesses.

Banks view these firms as too small or too manual to support flexible lending.


This capital mismatch creates a systemic failure where businesses with strong fundamentals cannot access the financial resources they need to evolve. Many of these target companies are lifestyle businesses—healthy, cash-flowing operations that support the owner's personal income and lifestyle, but underinvest in growth, systems, and equipment. These companies often prioritize stability over expansion and defer investments that could increase efficiency or scale.

The True Constraint: Capital Alignment, Not Market Demand

These firms are not failing due to poor products or a lack of demand. They are held back by a shortage of aligned capital and long-term support. The constraint is not market demand, but a lack of aligned capital and long-term operational vision. This misalignment creates a situation where thousands of viable businesses remain stuck in patterns of underinvestment, unable to modernize or scale despite having the fundamental customer relationships and operational knowledge to do so.
